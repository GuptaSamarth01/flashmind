Photosynthesis is the process by which green plants prepare their own food using sunlight, carbon dioxide, and water. This process mainly occurs in the leaves of plants, inside specialized organelles called chloroplasts.

Chlorophyll, the green pigment in plants, plays a crucial role in capturing sunlight. The energy from sunlight is used to convert carbon dioxide and water into glucose and oxygen.

The general equation for photosynthesis is:
6CO2 + 6H2O + sunlight → C6H12O6 + 6O2

Photosynthesis occurs in two main stages: the light-dependent reactions and the Calvin cycle. The light-dependent reactions capture energy from sunlight and convert it into chemical energy in the form of ATP and NADPH.

The Calvin cycle uses this chemical energy to convert carbon dioxide into glucose. This glucose is then used by the plant for growth and energy.

Oxygen is released as a byproduct of photosynthesis and is essential for the survival of most living organisms.

Factors affecting photosynthesis include light intensity, carbon dioxide concentration, temperature, and availability of water.

Plants are called autotrophs because they can produce their own food, unlike animals which are heterotrophs.

Photosynthesis is important because it maintains the balance of oxygen and carbon dioxide in the atmosphere and forms the base of the food chain.
